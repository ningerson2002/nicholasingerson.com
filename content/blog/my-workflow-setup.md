## Introduction

This article will be discussing my workflow setup as a computer science student at Allegheny College. Although I am not a professional programmer or software engineer, I believe it is beneficial for aspiring coders to consider the workflow setups of others. Most workflow setups are designed for experienced developers and may not be suitable for the average college student who works on a basic desk setup. Therefore, if you are a college student pursuing a computer science degree, this article may be of interest to you.

Keep in mind that my experience is heavily influenced by the curriculum at Allegheny College, which heavily focuses on Python and backend development. Additionally, my tools and machine setup are centered around coding in Python, R, and C++.

## Why Should You Care?

The short answer: you shouldn't. I'm not a professional programmer or software engineer, I'm a computer science student. However, I do think it's important for aspiring programmers to look to others' workflows for inspiration. Workflow setups are easy to find everywhere, but I've hardly seen any that are realistic to the average college student; someone who sits at a wooden desk with a hutch in an uncomfortable wooden chair all day.

So, if you happen to be someone who's going into college to pursue a computer science degree, this article may be just right for you.

## My Workflow Setup

**There are some things to keep in mind as you read this article:**

- As I've said, I'm not a professional programmer or software engineer.
- The curriculum at Allegheny College is very heavily Python-based and backend oriented. If you plan to go elsewhere, I would consider the type of curriculum you'll be going into.
- I mainly code in Python, R, and C++, so my tools will be oriented around those tools.

## My Machine

![My Setup](/blog/my-workflow-setup/workflow-1.jpg)

The machine is probably the most important part of any workflow setup. You need to be able to compile and execute code, and a subpar machine just won't let you do that. When looking for a machine, the CPU and Disk Space are the most important things to keep your eye on. I recommend using a machine with a decent CPU, 8 GB of RAM, and 256 GB of storage (preferably an SSD).

I'm currently using a [Dell XPS 13 9300](https://www.amazon.com/Dell-13-4-inch-InfinityEdge-Touchscreen-i7-1065G7/dp/B08LTNR3JZ/ref=sr_1_3?crid=1NSH6KD5DW498&keywords=dell+xps+13+9300&qid=1675187769&sprefix=dell+xps+13+9300%2Caps%2C152&sr=8-3) as my daily driver while I'm in school. It's got an Intel Core i5-1035GI @ 1.00 GHz x 8 CPU, 8 GB of RAM, and an SSD with 256.1 GB of space. In regards to peripherals, I recommend having a separate monitor, keyboard, and mouse. For example, I have my laptop set up on a stand off to the right with my monitor directly in front of me with my keyboard and mouse. This isn't a requirement, but I feel that it has made me much more productive and efficient.

## Windows, Linux, Mac? What OS Do I Use?

![Operating Systems](/blog/my-workflow-setup/workflow-2.jpg)

Well, if you read my blog on [why I switched to Linux from Windows](https://nicholasingerson.netlify.app/blog/why-i-code-on-linux/), you know that I'm more inclined to recommend Linux for programming. There are plenty of articles and YouTube videos you can watch on the subject, and I strongly recommend that you seek those out, do your own research, and choose the best operating system for _you_.

But if you're interested in my opinion, I can give it to you: Linux is my first choice, followed by Mac, and Windows is an OS that I simply wouldn't recommend. I should probably explain my reasoning behind these -- I find Mac to be a good alternative to Linux because of its similarity to Ubuntu and other Gnome Linux distros. The terminal, the UI, and the workflow are very similar as well. Finally, Windows is just a bad choice in my opinion. When something goes wrong on a Windows machine, it's difficult to figure out, while a Linux user can look into the source code of their distro. Once again, I suggest doing your own research on why Windows is bad (or good if that's how you roll).

### Okay, So I Use Linux... What About It?

When someone says that they use Linux, your next question should be "what distro?" There are several Linux distros that one can use -- Ubuntu, Pop!, Mint, Arch, etc. -- and they range in difficulty. I use Ubuntu which is widely regarded as the "most accessible" distribution, but it's important that you research what distrobution is right for you.

Further, after you've become comfortable with your distro, I suggest really diving into the system and customizing it to your liking!

## What About IDEs?

![IDE](/blog/my-workflow-setup/workflow-3.jpg)

In order to know which IDE to use, we have to understand what an IDE _is_. An IDE (Integrated Development Environment) is essentially what we use to write our code. For example, if you run a Windows system, you have Notepad by default. But if you're planning on taking computer science seriously, I strongly recommend that you look into IDEs and which would be best for you.

For my work so far, I have mainly been using VSCode as it has GitHub integration and many other features that make it good for my area of study. Recently, I have had to dip my toes into new IDEs for my Data Analytics and AI classes. For Data Analysis with R and Python, I use RStudio, and for working with Arduino boards in AI, I use the Arduino IDE.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, my workflow setup as a computer science student is centered around a Dell XPS 13 9300 laptop, Linux operating system and Visual Studio Code as my IDE. While my setup may not be suitable for every aspiring programmer, it serves as a good starting point for those who are looking for inspiration. The most important thing to keep in mind is to find the right machine, operating system and integrated development environment that works best for you. Whether it's Windows, Mac, or Linux, make sure to do your own research and choose what suits your needs the most. The ultimate goal is to have a productive and efficient setup that allows you to write and execute code effectively.
